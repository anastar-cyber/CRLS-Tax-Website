Module 1 — Foundations

Supply & Demand Basics

Before we get to taxes, let's understand the model that governs all markets.

📉 Demand Curve

As price goes up, buyers want less. As price falls, buyers want more. This inverse relationship creates a downward-sloping line.

Key idea: Buyers respond to prices. High prices = fewer purchases. Low prices = more purchases.
📈 Supply Curve

As price rises, producers want to sell more. As price falls, they supply less. This creates an upward-sloping line.

Key idea: Sellers respond to prices too. Higher prices = more profitable to produce = more supply.

Equilibrium
Equilibrium is the magic spot where supply equals demand — where the two curves cross. At this point, the market "clears": every buyer who wants to buy at that price can, and every seller who wants to sell at that price can.
Equilibrium Price (P*) Where S = D

The price at which quantity supplied = quantity demanded.

Equilibrium Quantity (Q*) Market Volume

Total amount of goods bought and sold at the equilibrium price.

⚠️ Out of Equilibrium

Surplus: Price too high → more supply than demand
Shortage: Price too low → more demand than supply

Equilibrium Point
Shifts vs. Movement along the curve:
Moving along a curve = responding to price changes.
Shifting the whole curve = something else changes (income, costs, preferences, technology).
↗ Demand Shifters
  • Income increases → demand shifts right
  • Substitute goods become cheaper → demand shifts left
  • Tastes & preferences change
  • Number of buyers changes
↗ Supply Shifters
  • Production costs fall → supply shifts right
  • Technology improves → supply shifts right
  • Input prices rise → supply shifts left
  • TAX on producers → supply shifts left ←

Key Concepts: Tax Incidence

💸 Who Really Pays?

Even though the tax is legally on consumers, the burden is shared. Producers receive less than before, consumers pay more. The split depends on elasticity.

📦 Deadweight Loss

Trades that would have happened without the tax no longer occur. This lost economic value — the deadweight loss — is a cost to society.

🏛️ Government Revenue

The rectangle between the old and new price × new quantity = tax revenue collected. This funds public goods like roads and schools.

Module 3 — Producer Tax

Tax on Producers

Now what if the government taxes sellers instead? The legal payer changes — but the economic outcome is surprisingly similar!

How a producer tax works:
A per-unit tax increases production costs. Producers need a higher price to supply the same quantity, so the supply curve shifts left (upward). Less is produced and sold.

⚖️ Consumer Tax vs. Producer Tax: Are They Different?

The Tax Equivalence Theorem: Economically, a tax on consumers and a tax on producers of the same amount produce identical outcomes — same equilibrium price wedge, same quantity, same deadweight loss, same government revenue. The only difference is who writes the check to the government.

Module 4 — Tax Systems

Types of Taxes & Income Tax

From sales tax to income tax, governments use many tools. Here's how they work — and who bears the burden.

📈
PROGRESSIVE

Progressive Tax

Higher earners pay a larger percentage of their income. The tax rate increases as income increases.

Earn $30k → pay 12%
Earn $200k → pay 32%
Earn $600k → pay 37%
📉
REGRESSIVE

Regressive Tax

Lower earners pay a larger share of their income in tax, even if the rate looks flat. Hurts the poor more.

Sales tax on food:
Poor family: 8% of income
Rich family: 0.3% of income
⚖️
PROPORTIONAL

Proportional (Flat) Tax

Everyone pays the same percentage regardless of income. Simple, but still debated for fairness.

Flat 20% for everyone:
Earn $50k → pay $10k
Earn $500k → pay $100k
🔑 Key insight: The U.S. federal income tax is progressive — it uses "tax brackets." You don't pay the top rate on all your income, only on the portion above each bracket threshold.
Income Tax Basics:
Income tax is a tax on money you earn — wages, salaries, business profits. In the U.S., the federal government uses a progressive bracket system. States have their own income taxes too (some states have none!).

Marginal vs. Effective Rate:
Marginal rate = the rate on your last dollar earned (your "bracket").
Effective rate = total tax ÷ total income. Always lower than marginal rate in a progressive system.
🛒
Sales Tax
REGRESSIVE

Added to purchases at checkout. Everyone pays the same rate, but it takes a bigger share of a poor person's income. Collected by states (0–10% range). Example: buying a $100 shirt in California → pay $10.25 in tax.

💰
Payroll Tax
REGRESSIVE

Funds Social Security & Medicare. Currently 7.65% from employees + 7.65% from employers (15.3% total). Only applies up to ~$168k of wages — so high earners pay a smaller share of their total income.

🏠
Property Tax
PROPORTIONAL

Based on assessed value of property. Main funding source for local schools and services. Can be regressive in practice because low-income owners may own in over-valued areas.

⛽
Excise Tax / Sin Tax
PER-UNIT

Taxes on specific goods: gasoline, tobacco, alcohol. Designed to reduce consumption of harmful goods and raise revenue. Works exactly like the supply-demand tax model — reduces quantity consumed.

📊
Capital Gains Tax
ON INVESTMENT PROFIT

Tax on profit from selling investments (stocks, real estate). Long-term gains (held 1+ year) taxed at lower rates (0%, 15%, 20%) than regular income. Major debate topic in tax policy.

🏦
Corporate Tax
ON BUSINESS PROFIT

Tax on business profits. U.S. federal rate is 21%. Economists debate whether corporations "really" pay this — they may pass it on as higher prices (consumer burden) or lower wages (worker burden).

Comparing the Tax Burden: A useful way to evaluate a tax is to ask — as income rises, does the tax rate rise, fall, or stay the same?
